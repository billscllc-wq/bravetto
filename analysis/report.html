The AI website monoculture—and how Bravetto breaks it

A read of how leading AI companies present themselves online, and where a warm, awake, principled brand can stand alone.

The category looks the same

See five AI homepages and you've seen the pattern. That sameness is the opening.

  • Palette: near-black or pure-white ground, one cool accent—blue, purple-blue gradient, or an acid-green pop. Cold and interchangeable.
  • Type: a neo-grotesque sans every time (Söhne, Inter, a custom grotesque). Almost never a serif, almost never editorial.
  • Hero: a big abstract claim—"frontier," "intelligence," "build"—over an abstract gradient or particle mesh, plus a product CTA.
  • Imagery: minimal or no human presence; abstract geometry.
  • Voice: "frontier," "build," "deploy," "scale." Capability shouted, warmth absent.

Recommendations

  1. Guard the warmth. The gold / ember / ink palette is the one thing no competitor has. Never let it drift toward the black-and-blue default.
  2. Lead with Abë as a presence. "Meet Abë. Say hello." is the rarest, strongest move in the category. Push it harder.
  3. Consider an editorial serif display face. It separates you from the grotesque monoculture and signals the content-design mind behind the brand.
  4. Keep the voice contrarian and plain. "Not artificial. Awake." works because everyone else says "frontier / build / scale." Never borrow their vocabulary.
  5. Make the guardian visible. A short principled section that owns the trust ground Anthropic frames coldly—with warmth and a human face.
  6. Avoid the gradient-mesh-on-black cliché. The semantic mesh escapes it only because it's warm and specific. Keep it gold-on-ink, never neon-on-black.
The category is a cold, grotesque, capability-shouting monoculture. Bravetto's edge is to be warm, named, awake, and principled—the AI company that feels like a mind you'd trust, not a platform you'd rent.
